# Part III — Core Principles

## Principle 1: Decisions Belong to Those Affected by Them

The closer a decision is to a person's daily life, the greater that person's right to influence it.

People living in a neighborhood understand local problems better than distant political institutions.

People using public transportation understand transportation issues better than individuals who never use the system.

People affected by environmental policies understand local environmental consequences better than remote administrators.

Governance should therefore begin at the level of impact.

### Example

A city intends to redesign a public square.

Under representative democracy:

- Citizens elect representatives.
- Representatives debate proposals.
- Citizens have little direct influence.

Under digital democracy:

- Residents of the affected district review proposals.
- Citizens submit alternatives.
- Costs are publicly visible.
- Residents vote directly on implementation.

Decision-making authority is aligned with those experiencing the outcome.

---

## Principle 2: Public Money Belongs to the Public

Government budgets are not abstract resources.

Every public budget originates from citizens through taxation, fees, or public assets.

Citizens should therefore possess continuous visibility into how public resources are allocated.

Transparency is not sufficient.

Citizens should also possess meaningful influence over spending priorities.

### Example

A municipality receives €100 million annually.

Instead of elected officials deciding all allocations internally, citizens participate in prioritization.

Residents may allocate preferences among:

- Road maintenance
- Public transportation
- Education
- Parks
- Housing
- Cultural programs

The resulting budget reflects collective priorities rather than political negotiation alone.

---

## Principle 3: Governance Should Be Issue-Based

Modern elections bundle hundreds of unrelated issues into a single vote.

A citizen may support:

- Lower taxes
- Strong environmental protection
- Expanded public transportation
- Decentralized government

No political party perfectly represents this combination.

As a result, elections force compromises before governance even begins.

Digital democracy separates issues.

Citizens evaluate individual proposals independently.

### Example

Instead of voting for a political party every four years, citizens vote separately on:

- A transportation proposal
- A healthcare proposal
- An education proposal
- A taxation proposal

Each decision stands on its own merits.

---

## Principle 4: Participation Requires Responsibility

Democracy functions best when participants understand the decisions they are making.

Complex issues often require technical knowledge.

Digital democracy should encourage informed participation rather than uninformed opinion.

The objective is not to restrict participation.

The objective is to increase decision quality.

### Example

A proposal concerns nuclear energy policy.

Before voting, citizens receive:

- Neutral educational material
- Expert arguments from multiple perspectives
- Economic impact assessments
- Environmental impact assessments

Citizens vote after reviewing the relevant information.

Participation becomes a process of learning rather than reaction.

---

## Principle 5: Expertise Should Inform, Not Rule

Experts possess valuable knowledge.

However, expertise alone should not grant political authority.

Likewise, public opinion alone should not replace expertise.

Effective governance combines both.

Experts provide information.

Citizens provide legitimacy.

### Example

Engineers may determine that three bridge designs are technically feasible.

Citizens decide which design best reflects community priorities and budget constraints.

Experts answer:

"Can this be done?"

Citizens answer:

"Should this be done?"

---

## Principle 6: Governance Should Be Continuous

Traditional elections occur every few years.

Problems emerge every day.

The gap between elections creates periods where citizens have little influence over evolving conditions.

Digital democracy enables continuous participation.

Governance becomes an ongoing process rather than a periodic event.

### Example

Residents identify increasing traffic congestion.

A proposal is submitted.

Discussion occurs publicly.

Cost estimates are published.

Voting occurs digitally.

Implementation begins.

The feedback cycle becomes months rather than years.

---

## Principle 7: Transparency Is the Default

Every significant decision should be traceable.

Citizens should be able to understand:

- What was proposed.
- Who supported it.
- How much it costs.
- How implementation progresses.
- Whether objectives were achieved.

Opacity creates opportunities for corruption, waste, and mistrust.

Transparency creates accountability.

### Example

A new school construction project is approved.

Citizens can observe:

- Original budget
- Contractors
- Construction timeline
- Cost changes
- Completion status

Public oversight becomes continuous rather than investigative.
