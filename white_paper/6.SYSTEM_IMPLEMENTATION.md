# Requirements Specification

# Module 1: Citizen Identity System

## Purpose

Establish a trusted mechanism that guarantees:

* One citizen = one account
* One citizen = one vote
* Eligibility verification
* Privacy protection
* Resistance to fraud
* Resistance to foreign interference

Without trusted identity, all other governance mechanisms become vulnerable.

---

# Goals

The Citizen Identity System shall:

1. Prevent duplicate identities.
2. Prevent fraudulent participation.
3. Preserve ballot secrecy.
4. Verify eligibility for participation.
5. Support accountability for public contributions.
6. Protect citizens from surveillance and coercion.
7. Remain auditable by independent parties.

---

# Risks

## Identity Fraud

An individual creates multiple accounts.

Impact:

* Vote manipulation
* Proposal manipulation

---

## Foreign Participation

Non-citizens attempt to influence decisions.

Impact:

* Sovereignty compromise

---

## Government Abuse

The identity system becomes a surveillance tool.

Impact:

* Political intimidation
* Loss of trust

---

## Platform Capture

System operators gain ability to manipulate identities.

Impact:

* Election fraud
* Selective exclusion

---

# Functional Requirements

## CI-001

Every citizen shall possess exactly one active civic identity.

---

## CI-002

Every civic identity shall be linked to a verified legal identity.

Verification methods may include:

* national identity systems
* passport verification
* government-issued credentials

---

## CI-003

Voting records shall be cryptographically separated from personal identity.

The system must verify eligibility without exposing vote choices.

---

## CI-004

Citizens shall be able to verify that their vote was counted.

Without revealing vote content.

---

## CI-005

All identity creation events shall be auditable.

---

## CI-006

Identity revocation procedures shall exist for:

* death
* loss of citizenship
* proven fraud

---

## CI-007

No administrator shall possess unilateral authority to disable citizen participation.

Multiple independent approvals required.

---

# Data Model

Citizen Identity contains:

* Citizen ID
* Citizenship status
* Residency information
* Jurisdiction memberships
* Participation permissions
* Optional expertise credentials

The following shall never be publicly visible:

* personal address
* voting history
* government identifiers

---

# Governance Requirements

Identity infrastructure shall be governed by:

* independent oversight board
* public audit process
* open technical specifications

No political party may control identity infrastructure.

---

# Success Criteria

* No duplicate identities
* No anonymous voting abuse
* Secret ballot maintained
* Public trust maintained
* Independent audits pass

# Module 3: Competency and Expertise System

## Purpose

The Competency System exists to improve decision quality without transferring political authority from citizens to experts.

The system recognizes that some citizens possess knowledge, experience, and skills relevant to specific policy domains.

Competency shall provide additional informational influence, not additional sovereignty.

Final authority remains with citizens.

---

# Core Principles

## Principle 1

All citizens are politically equal.

No citizen receives additional voting rights based solely on education, wealth, profession, status, or credentials.

---

## Principle 2

Competency should be visible.

Citizens evaluating proposals should be able to identify individuals with demonstrated knowledge in relevant domains.

---

Claims must be subject to public review and challenge.

Competency is domain-specific.

Competency does not transfer automatically between domains.

# Module 5: Decision Formation and Collective Agreement

## The Consensus Problem

A common criticism of participatory governance is that large groups struggle to reach agreement.

As the number of participants increases, so does the number of preferences, priorities, values, and interests.

Traditional governance often assumes that effective decision-making requires broad consensus before action can occur.

In practice, consensus becomes increasingly difficult as groups grow larger.

This creates a risk of:

* endless discussion,
* bureaucratic complexity,
* decision paralysis,
* political polarization,
* governance fatigue.

The Digital Democracy System does not assume that agreement is always possible.

Instead, it assumes that disagreement is a natural and permanent feature of society.

The purpose of governance is therefore not to eliminate disagreement.

The purpose of governance is to make collective decisions despite disagreement.

---

# Design Principle

Governance should focus on:

1. Identifying constraints.
2. Identifying objectives.
3. Generating alternatives.
4. Evaluating tradeoffs.
5. Selecting acceptable solutions.

The system should not require universal agreement.

It should require transparent decision-making.

---

# Constraint-Based Governance

## Problem

Political debates frequently begin with proposed solutions.

Participants immediately argue about competing ideas before establishing shared requirements.

This often produces conflict before common ground is identified.

---

## Solution

Every proposal begins with constraint identification.

Before discussing solutions, participants define what any acceptable solution must satisfy.

Examples:

Transportation Project

Constraints:

* Improve safety.
* Remain within budget.
* Maintain emergency vehicle access.

Education Reform

Constraints:

* Preserve educational quality.
* Remain accessible to all students.
* Stay within funding limits.

Budget Proposal

Constraints:

* Maintain essential services.
* Avoid unsustainable debt.
* Preserve constitutional obligations.

Once constraints are agreed upon, solution design becomes significantly more productive.

---

# Fundamental Principle

The system does not attempt to make all citizens agree.

Agreement is neither realistic nor necessary.

Instead, the system seeks to:

* make constraints explicit,
* generate alternatives,

# Deadlock Resolution Framework

The system assumes that some conflicts cannot be resolved through discussion alone.

When consensus is impossible, governance must still produce outcomes.

The objective of deadlock resolution is not to identify a perfect solution.

The objective is to identify the most legitimate, transparent, and least harmful path forward.

Deadlock resolution shall proceed through the following stages:

1. Constraint Analysis
2. Alternative Generation
3. Resource Partitioning
4. Compensation Assessment
5. Citizen Assembly Review
6. Escalation Review
7. Constitutional Review
8. Final Decision Procedure

No governance process shall remain indefinitely blocked.


# Module 6: System Integrity, Authority, and Anti-Capture Design

## Problem Statement: The Authority Paradox

Any governance system that enables collective decision-making must itself be implemented, maintained, and updated.

This creates a structural paradox:

* If the system has a final authority, that authority can be captured.
* If the system has no final authority, it may become unstable or unupdatable.

Therefore, the objective is not to define a single “final authority”, but to design a system in which **no single actor or aligned group can unilaterally control governance infrastructure**.

The goal is:

> No irreversible power concentration is possible within the system.

---

# Design Principle: Layered Accountability

Instead of centralized authority, the system is structured as multiple independent layers, each with limited and non-overlapping control.

No layer is sufficient on its own to alter governance outcomes.

---

# 1. Governance Infrastructure Layers

## 1.1 Protocol Layer (System Rules)

Defines:

* voting logic
* identity rules
* jurisdiction logic (sphere of impact)
* proposal lifecycle rules

### Properties:

* changes require supermajority approval
* changes require time-delayed execution
* changes require independent audit confirmation
* changes are publicly visible before activation

### Constraint:

No rapid modification of governance rules is permitted.

---

## 1.2 Implementation Layer (Operators)

Responsible for:

* running infrastructure
* maintaining software
* ensuring uptime
* executing approved actions

### Constraints:

* cannot modify governance rules
* cannot alter vote outcomes
* cannot change identity logic
* cannot override audit findings

Operators execute only what is already approved.

---

## 1.3 Audit Layer (Independent Verification)

Responsible for:

* monitoring system integrity
* verifying correctness of execution
* detecting fraud or manipulation
* publishing public reports

### Constraints:

* cannot execute changes
* cannot approve policy decisions
* cannot block citizen voting

Auditors observe and report only.

---

## 1.4 Citizen Layer (Sovereign Decision Layer)

Responsible for:

* proposing initiatives
* voting on policies
* reviewing proposals
* triggering audits
* approving system changes

Citizens retain final decision authority over policy.

---

# 2. Core Anti-Capture Principle

> No single layer can independently modify, execute, and validate governance actions.

Power is intentionally fragmented across independent roles.

---

# 3. Anti-Bribery and Anti-Capture Mechanisms

## 3.1 Transparent Action Logging

All governance-relevant actions are recorded in an immutable audit log:

* proposal creation
* voting outcomes
* system updates
* rule changes
* administrative actions

### Properties:

* publicly verifiable
* cryptographically secured
* append-only (no deletion without detection)

Voting privacy is preserved, but system actions are transparent.

---

## 3.2 Multi-Approval Governance Actions

Critical system changes require multiple independent approvals:

Example requirements:

* citizen supermajority approval
* independent audit confirmation
* multi-body governance endorsement

No single entity can enact structural change.

---

## 3.3 Rotating Randomized Roles

To prevent long-term capture:

* auditors are randomly selected
* oversight roles are time-limited
* governance assignments rotate continuously

### Purpose:

Break persistent bribery networks and reduce coordination stability.

---

## 3.4 Conflict-of-Interest Enforcement

Participants in governance infrastructure must declare:

* financial interests
* organizational affiliations
* indirect benefit exposure

### Enforcement:

Automatic exclusion from decisions where conflicts exist.

---

## 3.5 Standardized Compensation Model

All governance roles operate under:

* fixed, transparent compensation rules
* no discretionary bonuses
* no hidden rewards
* no private incentives

### Objective:

Eliminate financial leverage as a corruption vector.

---

## 3.6 Immutable Audit Trail

All system actions are stored in:

* append-only logs
* cryptographically verifiable records
* publicly inspectable history

### Effect:

Corruption cannot be erased, only exposed.

---

## 3.7 Delayed Execution of High-Impact Actions

Sensitive decisions include mandatory delay periods:

* public review window
* challenge period
* verification interval

### Purpose:

Prevent rapid capture or coordinated manipulation events.

---

## 3.8 Redundant Independent Auditing

Multiple independent audit bodies operate simultaneously:

* competing interpretations
* cross-validation of findings
* public disagreement resolution

### Purpose:

Avoid single-point failure in oversight.

---

# 4. Structural Guarantee: No Single Point of Authority

The system is designed so that:

* no individual controls rules
* no group controls execution alone
* no entity controls validation alone
* no actor can silently modify outcomes

All influence is distributed across independent mechanisms.

---

# 5. Core Security Principle

> Power is not eliminated. Power is decomposed.

Instead of concentrating authority in a single institution, the system ensures that:

* every powerful action requires coordination across independent actors
* coordination is observable
* coordination is costly to manipulate secretly
* coordination leaves permanent evidence

---

# 6. Design Objective

The system does not assume absence of corruption.

Instead, it ensures:

* corruption is fragmented
* corruption is detectable
* corruption is reversible or punishable
* corruption cannot remain hidden indefinitely

---

# 7. Fundamental Constraint

> No irreversible governance action shall be executable by a single entity or a single aligned control structure.

---

# 8. System Stability Principle

Governance stability is achieved not through trust in authorities, but through:

* structural separation of powers
* cryptographic transparency
* randomized oversight
* distributed accountability

---

# 9. Conclusion

This architecture replaces the concept of “final authority” with a system of layered constraints and distributed verification.

The legitimacy of governance does not depend on trust in any single institution.

It depends on the inability of any institution to gain unchecked control over the system.
